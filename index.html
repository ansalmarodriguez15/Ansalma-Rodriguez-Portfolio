Inspection Tool for 777X Heads-Up Display (HUD)

Skills Used

CATIA GD&T Precision Measurement Quality Assurance Design for Manufacturing (DFM)

Project Overview

During my internship at Boeing, I was assigned to design an inspection tool for the installation template of the 777X Heads-Up Display (HUD). While the specific engineering drawing and tolerances are confidential, the overall goal was to develop a system that verified the accuracy of the HUD’s hard-point alignment. This is a critical factor for ensuring proper optical projection and accuracy of the runway highlight feature.

Design Challenge

The HUD installation template includes several hard points that must remain within extremely tight tolerances, with the smallest allowable deviation being in the single digits of a thousandth of an inch.. My task was to create an inspection tool capable of detecting when any of those points were out of tolerance, without relying on complex or time-consuming CMM procedures.

Design Process

I approached the problem by designing a three-part inspection assembly that would interface directly with the existing hard points. The concept was based on laser alignment: one component housed a precision laser source, while the other two featured machined holes positioned parallel to each reference point.

In operation, the laser would pass cleanly through both holes if the points were within tolerance. If even slightly out of alignment, the beam would begin to clip the edge, which would show a visual indicator of deviation. Complete blockage indicated a critical out-of-tolerance condition requiring CMM verification and most likely a rework. This simple optical logic provided a non-contact and highly intuitive inspection method for mechanics to use before installation.

Iterative Refinement

Throughout the design process, I iterated multiple geometries to calibrate the tool’s detection sensitivity. My first results achieved a 0.076" deviation detection down and the last ones a 0.010". I also investigated using optical lenses to focus the beam for tighter detection thresholds. All CAD modeling was performed in CATIA V5, and I created testing blocks to simulate the HUD template for bench testing prior to full-scale implementation.

Once the initial design was validated virtually, I prepared the model for 3D printing to evaluate assembly alignment and beam behavior. Although the final machining phase was pending at the end of my internship, I documented a full continuation guide outlining dependent and independent variables, material considerations, and calibration methodology for the next engineer to take advantage of the momentum and carry the project forward.

Outcome & Impact

The project demonstrated how optical inspection tools can be designed to achieve high accuracy while remaining lightweight and easy to use on the shop floor. It also gave me hands-on experience in tolerance stack-up analysis, fixture design, and DFM principles. Everything within the context of Boeing’s rigorous engineering environment, and a reach to perfection.

Economy Seat Tray

Skills Used

CATIA Prototyping & Fabrication Design for User Experience Design for Manufacturing (DFM)

Project Overview

As part of Boeing’s 3-day BCA ONE Intern Design-A-Thon, I joined a team of three to tackle a challenge centered around improving the passenger flight experience. Our goal was to enhance comfort and convenience for economy-class passengers while ensuring airlines could adopt the design without major cost increases.

After analyzing common passenger frustrations, we discovered that one of the most frequent (yet highly overlooked) issues comes from the economy seat tray’s cup holder. It’s often too shallow to actually hold drinks, causing spills and stress during turbulence. This creates a poor passenger experience and, in some cases, safety concerns from spilled hot beverages.

Concept Development

With that insight, we reimagined the existing seat tray by integrating a collapsible cup holder inspired by hiking and camping cup designs. The collapsible feature allows it to fit within the tray’s original dimensions when stowed, yet securely hold a range of drink sizes when deployed. This design not only prevents spills but also provides a containment area in case of minor leaks.

We further improved usability by adding a small front tab that serves as a phone rest, allowing passengers to comfortably watch content, which aligns with airlines’ shift toward in-flight streaming services. The bottom of the collapsible holder can be customized with airline branding and colors, creating a new space for identity and personalization without additional parts or materials.

Design Strategy

One of our main constraints was ensuring the design remained retrofit-compatible. By maintaining the original tray dimensions and hinge system, airlines could easily replace existing trays with our new design once their current ones reach end-of-life, avoiding the need for large-scale retooling or redesign. This decision balanced innovation and practicality, making the design both attractive and feasible for manufacturers.

Prototyping & Results

Given the short timeline, we worked efficiently by brainstorming, designing, and fabricating a 1:1 functional prototype within just a day and a half at Boeing’s Integration Center. The prototype showcased the collapsible mechanism, phone stand, and overall ergonomics of the design. We concluded the event by presenting our final concept to a panel of Boeing engineers and designers, earning 2nd place in the competition for innovation and user-centered design.

Reflection

This project reinforced the importance of balancing creativity with manufacturability. It also showed how user-centered thinking can uncover meaningful improvements in everyday designs, even in something as common as an airplane seat tray. Beyond the competition, this experience strengthened my skills in rapid ideation, teamwork, and communicating design intent under tight deadlines.

ATV Avionics Enclosure

Skills Used

OnShape Laser Cutting Thermal Management & Airflow Design for Manufacturing (DFM)

Project Overview

As part of the ASCEND Autonomous Systems Research Team, I was responsible for designing an avionics enclosure for our ATV’s critical flight electronics, including the Pixhawk flight controller, multiple Raspberry Pis, GPS module, and supporting communication interfaces. The primary goal was to develop a compact, modular, and EMI-protected housing that safeguarded sensitive electronics while fitting seamlessly within the limited space of the vehicle chassis.

Design Challenge

The enclosure had to satisfy multiple engineering constraints simultaneously: electromagnetic compatibility between high-frequency components (such as the Raspberry Pi) and navigation systems, thermal regulation to prevent overheating, structural integrity to withstand vibration and impact, and spatial efficiency to integrate within the ATV’s frame. Furthermore, each subsystem required clear wire routing and access for maintenance without compromising protection.

Design Process

I began by modeling each electronic component in SolidWorks and mapping the physical wiring layout to ensure optimal cable management and clearance. The design evolved into a multi-compartment box, where each module—Pixhawk, Raspberry Pi, GPS, and power converters—occupied a dedicated block within the structure to minimize EMI coupling.

The enclosure was constructed using laser-cut acrylic panels joined with a modified dovetail-style interlocking joint for added rigidity and ease of assembly. To facilitate airflow, I patterned two of the four sides with ventilation hole arrays sized to balance cooling and dust protection. The bottom panel was engraved with the wiring diagram layout, providing a built-in guide for organized cable routing and component placement.

The top lid incorporated a raised GPS mounting tab to expose the antenna to open air—maintaining direct satellite visibility while keeping the rest of the electronics enclosed. This design choice preserved both performance and safety by isolating the GPS from EMI sources while optimizing reception.

Prototyping & Fabrication

Once the 3D model was validated through spatial fit checks, I exported the 2D profiles for laser cutting and assembled the prototype to verify dimensional accuracy and airflow effectiveness. The modular dovetail joints proved robust under vibration tests, while the engraved wiring map significantly streamlined installation and troubleshooting.

Outcome & Impact

The final design provided a lightweight, EMI-resistant, and serviceable avionics housing that integrated seamlessly into the ASCEND ATV platform. It improved cable management efficiency, reduced EMI interference with the GPS subsystem, and enhanced airflow to maintain electronic reliability during extended testing sessions. The enclosure design has since been adopted as a standard reference architecture for future ASCEND vehicle builds, demonstrating a balance of precision engineering and practical manufacturability.

ATV Gas Motor Removal

Skills Used

Mechanical Disassembly System Layout & Optimization

Project Overview

As part of the ASCEND Autonomous Systems Research Team, our group was tasked with transitioning our Autonomous Test Vehicle (ATV) from a gasoline-powered drivetrain to a fully electric propulsion system. This conversion was required by the university to allow experimental testing and research operations on the main campus, where the use of internal combustion engines was restricted for safety and environmental reasons.

Team Objective

Working as part of a three-person mechanical integration team, I helped lead the effort to remove the existing gas engine while maintaining the structural and electrical integrity of the platform. The objective was to prepare the vehicle chassis for the upcoming electric motor retrofit, ensuring that all components—fuel lines, throttle linkage, exhaust, and engine mounts—were safely extracted and documented for future reinstallation reference.

Process & Safety

The removal process began with a detailed system review of the ATV’s fuel and electrical subsystems to identify all active circuits and potential hazards. I assisted in depressurizing the fuel system, disconnecting the battery, and isolating all power sources prior to disassembly. We used tagout and labeling procedures to track every cable, bracket, and mounting bolt to maintain full traceability during the conversion.

Once the engine assembly was disconnected, our team coordinated the mechanical removal using a hoist and custom sling setup to ensure even load distribution. After extraction, we performed a clean decontamination of the chassis to remove residual fuel and oil, making the frame safe for electronics installation.

Outcome & Preparation for Electrification

The gas engine removal was successfully completed without any structural damage or contamination incidents. The cleaned and documented chassis was officially cleared for use in the electrification phase of the ASCEND project. This operation provided hands-on experience in mechanical teardown, safety planning, and cross-disciplinary coordination—essential skills for future vehicle design and propulsion research.

Beyond the technical accomplishment, the project marked a major milestone in ASCEND’s sustainability transition, paving the way for the development of a zero-emission test platform capable of campus-based autonomous experimentation.

ATV Electrical Motor Implementation

Skills Used

Welding Prototyping Structural Design System Integration

Project Overview

As part of the ASCEND Autonomous Systems Research Team, I contributed to the design and implementation of the electric motor system for the ATV, marking a major milestone in our transition from combustion to electric propulsion. This phase focused on selecting an appropriate motor, designing an efficient mounting system under strict space and voltage constraints, and preparing the ATV for integration with the new powertrain architecture.

Research & Motor Selection

We began by researching available electric motors capable of providing sufficient power to reach the ATV’s target speed of approximately 30 mph. The challenge was identifying a model that balanced performance, efficiency, and cost, while also complying with the UT campus safety guidelines limiting us to a low-voltage configuration (four 5V batteries in parallel).

After evaluating several commercial options, our team selected a compact brushed DC motor that met the torque and speed requirements while remaining within the electrical and budget constraints. Once chosen, we searched for compatible custom mounting brackets to secure the cylindrical motor in a flat, stable orientation.

Design & Mounting Strategy

We explored multiple mounting configurations to integrate the motor within the limited space of the ATV’s chassis. Early prototypes were constructed using cardboard and mockups to test various orientations and attachment methods. However, during physical prototyping we discovered that several of these designs interfered with the gear chain alignment, causing collisions with the ATV frame due to insufficient height clearance.

To overcome this, I proposed and helped develop a suspended motor mount system — a novel approach that allowed the motor to hang securely from the frame rather than sit atop it. This configuration freed up valuable interior space for critical components such as the motor controller, avionics, and battery modules, while maintaining precise chain alignment and minimizing vibration.

Prototype & Structural Integration

The final design consisted of a pair of C-channel aluminum rails supporting the custom motor plate. These rails acted like a drawer system, enabling slight sliding adjustment during alignment and maintenance. During the initial prototype phase, the motor was temporarily supported with a steel cable harness to test stability and clearance before committing to permanent fabrication.

Once the mechanical lead approved the concept, we planned the final integration process. Due to extreme space limitations, traditional fasteners such as bolts and screws were impractical. Instead, we used a welded reference plate to join the C-channels directly to the ATV frame. This method ensured the rails remained perfectly parallel and rigid, maintaining the correct spacing for the motor mount while resisting operational vibration.

Outcome & Impact

The implemented design provided a lightweight, compact, and serviceable solution for housing the new electric motor. The suspended mounting system maximized frame space utilization, improved drivetrain alignment, and simplified future maintenance and upgrades. Also, we tested our ATV to reach 20mph at 25% throttle, which leads us to believe we can achieve 40mph at full throttle!

This project strengthened my experience in mechanical design under constraints, hands-on prototyping, and systems integration. It also demonstrated how creative thinking — such as transforming a traditional rigid mount into a suspended system — can turn space limitations into engineering innovation.

Robotic Hand

Skills Used

OnShape Robotics Mechanism Design Prototyping Design for Manufacturing

Project Overview

Motivated by a deep curiosity for humanoid robotics and accessible engineering design, I initiated a self-driven project to create a 3D-printed robotic hand that could be easily fabricated and adapted for use in assistive technology applications. The long-term vision is to share the design with ADAPT Texas, a student organization dedicated to developing custom-engineered devices for the disabled community at no cost.

Design Concept

The robotic hand was modeled entirely in OnShape using a biomimetic design approach inspired by the human hand’s tendon-driven motion. Instead of using rigid linkages, I implemented fishing line–based tendon cables routed through each finger and anchored to a bar at the fingertip. This design allows the fingers to flex and retract naturally, replicating the curling motion of human digits when tensioned.

The first design iteration placed all actuators within the palm, directly controlling the cable tension for each finger. However, during mechanical testing and CAD analysis, I found that this configuration compromised the palm’s strength and internal space. To improve load distribution and durability, I relocated the actuators to the forearm assembly, which not only enhanced structural integrity but also freed up space for future sensor integration and improved wiring management.

Engineering Development

The current CAD assembly includes all mechanical linkages, cable routing paths, and potential mounting points for electronics and actuators. My ongoing research focuses on sensor integration to enable tactile feedback and position control, with candidates including:

  • Load cells for force feedback at fingertip joints
  • Potentiometers to measure angular displacement
  • EMG (electromyography) sensors for detecting muscle signals from the user
  • Temperature sensors for thermal sensitivity applications

Beyond the hardware, the design requires careful material selection to balance strength, flexibility, and affordability. I have been experimenting with PLA and TPU for the joints and tendons, respectively, to emulate human biomechanics while maintaining accessibility for low-cost fabrication.

Research Challenges & Future Work

This project has involved extensive research into actuator placement trade-offs, space constraints, and the integration of sensors into small-scale mechanisms. The goal is to develop a platform that bridges robotics research and real-world assistive design — one that can be customized, printed, and assembled by students and engineers seeking to create affordable prosthetic or robotic systems.

Outcome & Impact

The project is still in active development, but it has already provided valuable insights into mechanical design optimization, additive manufacturing techniques, and human–machine interaction principles. By open-sourcing the final design to ADAPT Texas, this robotic hand aims to serve as both a teaching platform for engineering students and a functional assistive device prototype that promotes accessibility and inclusivity through engineering innovation.

Mini Bowling - Table Game

Skills Used

Mechanism Kinematics Kinematic Analysis Matlab Python

Project Overview

As the final project for my Robotic Mechanism Design course, our team was challenged to design and prototype a complex linkage mechanism capable of producing a controlled and purposeful motion. We developed a mini bowling table game driven by a four-bar linkage that mimicked the coordinated motion of a human bowler’s arm and wrist — combining spin, flick, and follow-through motions to accurately launch a miniature bowling ball toward the pins.

Concept & Design Motivation

We began by studying the biomechanics of a bowling throw and translating those into mechanical linkages that could achieve similar kinematic profiles. Using MotionGen, we modeled and optimized linkage dimensions to replicate the spin and whip action seen in a bowler’s arm. Our final design satisfied the Grashof condition for continuous motion and maintained a 1-DoF configuration, ensuring that a single rotary input could drive the entire throwing motion.

The mechanism’s geometry was tuned to convert the crank rotation into a realistic curved trajectory at the end-effector, producing a consistent “release” point with angular acceleration suitable for accurate bowling throws.

Mathematical & Simulation Analysis

To validate the theoretical design, we performed a full kinematic analysis in MATLAB. By solving the geometric constraint equations of the four-bar linkage, we simulated and animated the motion profile, confirming the expected combination of spin and whip dynamics. We also analyzed the position, velocity, and acceleration of the linkage’s tip — the point representing the “hand” of the bowler — to confirm a smooth release curve and proper timing for the flick motion.

Actuation & Control

The linkage was powered by a rotary actuator programmed to complete exactly one revolution per button press. This ensured that each throw was consistent and that the system would reset automatically for the next bowl. Adjusting the actuator’s speed allowed us to control the throwing strength while maintaining mechanical safety.

“Smile & Frown” Release Mechanism

To accurately replicate the human wrist flick, we designed a custom scoop-shaped end effector, nicknamed the “Smile & Frown”. The scoop’s rear section followed a concave “smile” ) profile that held the bowling ball securely, while the front “frown” ( curve released the ball at the precise moment of maximum angular velocity. The design followed an inverse ramp profile to delay release until optimal momentum was achieved, ensuring smooth and consistent throws.

Fabrication & Material Selection

The prototype was constructed using a combination of laser-cut acrylic and wood for structural balance and surface optimization. The linkages and the top surface of the bowling alley were laser-cut from acrylic to take advantage of its low friction coefficient compared to wood, allowing the ball to roll smoothly with minimal resistance. The base of the bowling lane was made from wood to provide better stability, vibration damping, and support for the entire setup.

The bowling lane also featured a built-in gutter system to prevent the ball from leaving the track, improving playability and experimental consistency. Finally, the pins were 3D printed using a Raise3D printer, allowing for uniform geometry and scalable replication for future class projects.

Outcome & Reflection

The final mechanism successfully reproduced the combined spin and flick motion of a human bowler. The simulated results aligned closely with physical testing, validating both the mathematical model and fabrication accuracy. The system’s repeatable control and realistic motion made it one of the most dynamic mechanisms in the course.

This project strengthened my understanding of kinematic synthesis, mechanical actuation, and material-based design decisions. It was also an excellent demonstration of how theory, simulation, and fabrication can integrate to produce a precise and engaging robotic mechanism.

RC Car

Skills Used

SolidWorks Motor Integration Python

Project Overview

As part of the Robotic Mechanism Design course, my team, “The Decepticons”, was challenged to design and build a remote-controlled vehicle using a set of two powered actuators and one non-actuated wheel. The project combined mechanical design, circuit integration, and programming to develop a functional RC car capable of both manual and autonomous motion.

Mechanical Design

Our first step was to model the vehicle chassis in SolidWorks, ensuring it provided adequate space for the motors, control board, and battery pack while maintaining a lightweight structure for efficient motion. The design included precise cutouts for wiring and wheel placement, as well as a flat upper deck for mounting electronics.

To personalize our build, we incorporated the Decepticons logo into the chassis design, which became both a visual centerpiece and a representation of our team’s identity. Once finalized, the chassis was laser-cut from acrylic for high dimensional accuracy and durability, providing a clean, transparent look that showcased the internal electronics.

Electrical & Control System

We designed and assembled the electrical circuit responsible for driving the two actuator-powered wheels. The system included a microcontroller board, motor drivers, and a compact power distribution layout to ensure stable operation under load.

For control, we programmed the system to operate in two modes:

  • Manual Mode: Controlled via a joystick interface, allowing direct control over steering and acceleration.
  • Autonomous Mode: Pre-programmed to drive in continuous circles using a fixed control algorithm that modulated wheel speeds asymmetrically.

We tuned the actuation timing and response curves to ensure stable circular motion and prevent slippage during turns. The circuit was compact, modular, and easy to reconfigure for future testing or additional sensors.

Fabrication & Assembly

The entire structure was laser-cut from acrylic sheets and assembled using friction-fit tabs and fasteners. This approach allowed easy maintenance and rapid iteration during testing. The acrylic chassis not only provided a professional finish but also allowed us to visualize and debug the electronic connections in real time.

Outcome & Reflection

The final RC car successfully met all project requirements and exceeded expectations with its reliable circular motion and responsive joystick control. The integrated design showcased a complete mechatronic system — blending mechanical CAD modeling, electrical integration, and control programming.

This project deepened my understanding of motor control systems, mechanical–electrical co-design, and real-time motion tuning. It also emphasized the value of teamwork and creativity in engineering design, especially when transforming basic components into a fully functional and interactive robotic system.

Slider Crank Mechanism

Skills Used

Mechanism Design SolidWorks Prototyping & Fabrication

Project Overview

As part of a hands-on module in Robotic Mechanism Design, I constructed a slider-crank mechanism capable of manually lifting a one-pound weight. The purpose of this project was to explore the mechanical efficiency and motion transfer of simple linkages and understand the inherent limitations and inefficiencies that arise in real-world assembly.

Design & Objective

The task focused on creating a functional slider-crank system that could be actuated by hand while maintaining minimal joint play and frictional losses. I designed the linkage to demonstrate how small imperfections or tolerances could amplify motion irregularities, providing a tangible understanding of why precision alignment and stiffness are critical in practical mechanism design.

Performance & Optimization

After assembling and calibrating the system, we achieved smooth, consistent motion with remarkably low mechanical backlash. By carefully selecting pivot clearances and verifying alignment through iterative fitting, we reduced unwanted vibration and achieved a rigid transmission of force, which allowed the hand-actuated crank to efficiently lift the 1 lb load with minimal slippage.

Outcome & Reflection

While simple in construction, the project highlighted the trade-offs between theoretical linkage efficiency and real-world mechanical losses. It reinforced the importance of material rigidity, tolerance matching, and proper joint design in mechanical systems. This exercise served as a practical foundation for more complex linkage projects like the Mini Bowling Game, where dynamic forces and motion precision became key.
